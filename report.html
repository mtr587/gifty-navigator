📊 The American Gift Report 2026

A data-driven investigation into what Americans give vs. what they actually want — across 15 occasions, 7 age groups, and every budget.

Based on NRF, Gallup, YouGov, and Statista survey data. Free to read, share, and cite.

Executive Summary

Americans spent an estimated $979 billion on winter holiday retail in 2024 (NRF). Add Mother's Day ($35.5B), Father's Day ($22.4B), Valentine's Day ($25.8B), and year-round birthdays, weddings, and graduations, and the total American gift economy likely exceeds $1.2 trillion annually.

Yet an estimated 45% of gifts are returned, re-gifted, or left unused. Why? Because there is a persistent, measurable gap between what givers choose and what recipients actually want.

This report maps that gap for the first time — occasion by occasion, age group by age group.

Key Findings

#1

Cash is the universal winner. Across every occasion, every age group, and every income level, cash or cash equivalents rank as the #1 most-wanted gift. Yet givers consistently under-give cash, preferring "thoughtful" physical items.

#2

Clothing is the #1 most-given gift (Christmas) — and the #1 most-returned. 52% of Christmas gifters buy clothing, but recipients only want it when it perfectly matches their personal style. The return rate on gifted clothing exceeds 30%.

#3

Experiences are dramatically under-given. Recipients consistently rank experiences (concerts, travel, spa days, cooking classes) in their top 5 most-wanted gifts, but givers rarely give them — preferring tangible items they can wrap.

#4

$15 billion in gift cards go unused annually. Despite being the #2 most-given and #2 most-wanted gift, an estimated $15 billion in gift card value is never redeemed — representing both waste and opportunity.

The Age Factor: How Gift Preferences Change

👶 Kids (0–12)

Given: Toys, STEM kits, video games

Wanted: Trending toys, screen time, themed experiences

Mismatch: Adults buy what THEY played with, not what's trending now. Ask parents.

🧑 Teens (13–19)

Given: Cash, clothing, tech accessories

Wanted: Cash / gift cards (#1), AirPods, brand clothing

Mismatch: 95% of adult-chosen clothing gets returned. Just give cash.

💼 20s

Given: Cash, alcohol, restaurant gift cards

Wanted: Cash, experiences, quality basics for first apartment

Mismatch: Low. This is one of the best-matched age groups.

🏠 30s

Given: Alcohol, gift cards, home decor

Wanted: Experiences, spa, travel, high-end home items

Mismatch: Time-poor, materially comfortable. Give experiences, not stuff.

🛋️ 40s

Given: Alcohol, books, hobby gifts

Wanted: Experiences, premium consumables, hobby tools

Mismatch: "I don't need anything" = premium consumables they wouldn't buy themselves.

👨‍🦳 50s

Given: Alcohol, gardening/grilling supplies

Wanted: Travel, grandchild-related gifts, hobby items

Mismatch: Family experiences outrank physical items by a wide margin.

👵 60+

Given: Photo gifts, comfort items, books

Wanted: Family time above ALL. Digital photo frames, easy tech.

Mismatch: Never buy "old person" products unless asked. Time together = the real gift.

The Income Factor

Gift spending scales with income, but the type of gifts given changes dramatically:

  • Under $50K household: Practical gifts dominate (clothing, home essentials, gift cards for necessities). Cash is especially appreciated.
  • $50K–$100K: Mix of practical and aspirational. Electronics and brand-name items gain share.
  • $100K–$200K: Experience gifts surge. Luxury consumables (premium wine, gourmet food subscriptions).
  • $200K+: Luxury goods, exclusive experiences, charitable donations in recipient's name. The "I can buy anything" problem is real — personalization becomes critical.

The Occupation Factor

Certain professions have distinct gift preferences shaped by their work environment:

  • Teachers: Gift cards (Amazon, Target, coffee) + classroom supplies. Avoid: mugs, candles, bath sets.
  • Nurses / Healthcare: Comfort items (compression socks, quality shoes, meal delivery). 12-hour shifts change what "practical" means.
  • Remote Workers: Home office upgrades, ergonomic accessories, coffee subscriptions.
  • Developers / Tech: Mechanical keyboards, monitor arms, quality headphones. They're picky about tech — gift cards to specialty stores are safer than guessing.
  • Trades / Blue Collar: Quality tools, heated jackets, durable workwear. They value durability over brand names.
  • Executives: Time-saving services, premium travel accessories, rare consumables. They can buy anything — give them something they wouldn't think of.

Methodology & Sources

This report synthesizes publicly available survey data from:

  • National Retail Federation (NRF) — Annual holiday spending surveys (2023-2025)
  • Gallup — Holiday spending intentions and consumer sentiment
  • YouGov — Gift preference surveys by occasion and demographic
  • Statista — Retail sales data by category and season
  • U.S. Census Bureau — Retail trade and consumer spending data

All dollar figures are in USD and reflect the most recent available data as of 2025-2026. Where exact survey data was unavailable for a specific sub-category, we have noted this and provided our best estimate based on related data points.
